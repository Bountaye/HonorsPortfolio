BallMapper Paper — rho knot invariant

First research project (Fall 2024)

This was my first ever research project at NC state, and first research project ever! I began research in the Fall of 2024, with my research mentor Dr Radmila Sazdanovic, and decided to take her special topics course about the topic in Spring 2025. I stumbled into the course wildly underqualified, sitting amongst almost exclusively graduate students who were well versed in Topology - the field of study in question. Eventually, through consistent effort and countless hours, I produced this paper. The topic of this paper was the rho knot invariant, and the tool I used was BallMapper, a topological data analysis tool.

Final reflection

A culminating reflection of my experience with the UHP

If I had to summarize my time at NC State in one sentence, it would probably be: I kept accidentally putting myself in rooms where I felt underqualified, and then somehow surviving.

When I started college, I didn’t have some grand master plan. I knew I liked math. I knew engineering sounded cool. Computer science felt practical. That was about it. What I didn’t expect was how much my time here would change the way I think, the way I approach challenges, and honestly, the way I see myself.

The BallMapper research project is probably the clearest example of that. I walked into a topology special topics course in Spring 2025 surrounded almost entirely by graduate students. These were people who actually knew what they were doing. I absolutely did not. I had never done research before. I barely understood the vocabulary. But instead of dropping the class (which was very tempting), I decided to just… stay. I asked questions. I reread papers I didn’t understand the first time. I spent way too many hours trying to wrap my head around the rho knot invariant and how BallMapper could be used as a tool to study it. Eventually, all that confusion turned into a paper. My first paper. That experience taught me something simple but important: feeling behind doesn’t mean you don’t belong. It usually just means you’re learning.

MA225, my proofs class, was the first time math stopped being about “getting the answer” and started being about actually understanding something. Before that, math was mostly calculus and computation. Proof-writing forced me to slow down and think abstractly. It was frustrating at first. But that shift in thinking ended up influencing everything else I did, research, coding, even how I explain ideas. Looking back at my old proof portfolio now, some of it feels amateurish, but it represents the moment my brain started thinking like a mathematician instead of just a student trying to do well on exams.

Then there’s computer science. CSC116 was my first real coding project, Battleship. It wasn’t glamorous. It probably wasn’t beautifully written. But it was the first time I created something from scratch and thought, “Wait, this actually works.” Fast forward a few years to CSC326 and WolfCafe, and the difference is almost funny. WolfCafe was a full team project, complete with design discussions, backend logic, frontend development, and all the chaos that comes with five people trying to build something together. That class taught me that software development is as much about communication as it is about code. It also made me realize I genuinely enjoy building systems, not just solving isolated problems.

Some of my favorite growth didn’t even happen in academic settings. Becoming President of the Table Tennis Club in August 2025 was something I didn’t originally plan on. I joined the club because I liked playing. Over two years, it turned into something much bigger. Regionals at Duke became one of the highlights of my year, waking up early, driving down together, playing matches, grabbing lunch, and just spending time with people who slowly became close friends. As president, I had to step up in ways I hadn’t before. Organizing, communicating, making decisions, it wasn’t just about showing up and playing anymore. It made me realize I’m capable of leading without trying to become someone I’m not.

The Guitar Ensemble class might honestly be one of my favorite memories at NC State. I bought my electric guitar during a summer when I was basically living with just my climbing wall and my thoughts. I practiced constantly. When I signed up for MUS160, I didn’t expect much, but performing at the end of the semester changed something. Playing “Sweet Child o’ Mine” and “Billie Jean” in front of a crowd of my friends was terrifying for about 30 seconds… and then it was just awesome. That moment reminded me that growth isn’t always intellectual. Sometimes it’s just about putting yourself in situations that scare you a little and seeing what happens.

Even the earlier engineering projects, like the Compact Diagnosis Unit in E102, mean something to me now. Was it realistic? Absolutely not. Was it kind of ridiculous in hindsight? Definitely. But it was my first introduction to engineering design. The chicken coop project in E101, complete with an automated curtain-motor door, was my first experience actually building something physical. Those projects weren’t perfect, but they represent the beginning. And I like that.

The Race, Membership, and Eugenics project also stands out because it forced me to think about things I had never seriously examined before. Learning about the eugenics movement and interviewing students at HBCUs gave me perspectives I didn’t grow up with. It reminded me that education isn’t just technical. It’s social. It’s historical. It’s human.

If there’s a pattern in all of this, it’s that I kept leaning into things that felt slightly uncomfortable. A graduate-level math course. A leadership position. A live performance. A team of strangers building software. Every time, I felt some version of “I don’t know if I’m ready for this.” And every time, I came out more capable than I thought I was.

I’m graduating soon, and I still don’t have everything figured out. But I’m okay with that now. NC State didn’t just teach me math, engineering principles, or Java and React. It taught me that growth usually looks like confusion at first. It taught me that friendships built over tournaments and late-night debugging sessions matter. It taught me that sometimes the “unrealistic” freshman project is just the first step toward something much bigger.

Most importantly, it taught me that I can walk into a room where I feel underqualified… and stay anyway.
